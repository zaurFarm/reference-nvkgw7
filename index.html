From Pepsi to Anthracite: A Look at Rolex's Famous Ceramic Bezels

Before 2005, Rolex bezels were anodized aluminum — lightweight, easy to scratch, and prone to fading. The introduction of Cerachrom ceramic changed everything. The material is virtually scratchproof, UV-resistant, and maintains its color indefinitely. Every Rolex sport watch bezel made since 2013 uses Cerachrom, and the color palette has expanded steadily.

The iconic colorways

The black Cerachrom bezel appeared first on the Submariner in 2010. Blue followed on the Submariner ref. 116619 in white gold. The red-and-blue Pepsi bezel on the GMT-Master II debuted in 2018 and became one of the most recognizable watch design elements of the decade. The black-and-brown Root Beer, the black-and-blue Batman, and the green Submariner Starbucks rounded out the lineup.

How Rolex achieves two-tone ceramic

Producing a two-tone ceramic bezel requires depositing two different mineral compositions into a single disc and firing them together. The boundary between colors must be sharp at the twelve-o'clock position and remain consistent across every unit. Rolex reportedly had a rejection rate above fifty percent during early Pepsi production. Current yields are significantly better but still lower than monochrome bezels.

The 2026 additions

This year introduced Anthracite — a dark metallic grey with a faint purple undertone that appears on the new Yacht-Master and a special Datejust configuration. Rolex also debuted a gradient green-to-black bezel on the 2026 Submariner that fades seamlessly across the sixty-minute scale. The gradient technique is new for Cerachrom and suggests Rolex is pushing the material science further than anyone expected.
